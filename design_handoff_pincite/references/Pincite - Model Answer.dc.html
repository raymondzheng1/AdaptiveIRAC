Question · Torts — NegligenceHypothetical · 10 marks

Marko dives from a council-managed jetty into a swimming hole and strikes a submerged sandbar, sustaining a serious neck injury. The depth was not marked and no warning was displayed, though locals dived there often. Advise whether the Council owed Marko a duty of care and whether it breached that duty.

Issue

Whether the Council owed Marko a duty of care in respect of the unmarked depth at the swimming hole, and if so, whether it breached that duty by failing to warn.

Rule

A duty of care arises where the defendant ought reasonably to have foreseen that carelessness on its part could injure a class of persons including the plaintiff . An occupier that controls land owes entrants a duty to take reasonable care to avoid foreseeable risks of injury . Breach is determined by the calculus of negligence: the probability and likely seriousness of the harm weighed against the burden of precautions and the social utility of the activity .

Application

Swimmers habitually used the jetty, so injury from diving into water of unknown depth was reasonably foreseeable — it was not far-fetched or fanciful . A comparable swimming-hazard duty was recognised where an authority invited public use of a waterway . The gravity of a neck injury is high, while the burden of a depth sign was slight; a reasonable authority would have taken that precaution.

Conclusion

The Council owed Marko a duty of care and, on the balance of the calculus, likely breached it by failing to warn of the unmarked depth. Final liability remains subject to causation and any reduction for contributory negligence.
